Where do we draw the line? Exploring similarities in different handwriting styles

During National Inclusion Week, we collected samples of handwriting styles across The Alan Turing Institute of the phrase Each One Reach One, the theme of the week.

We've trained a convolutional autoencoder to learn a compressed encoding of each sample and used dimensionality reduction algorithms (UMAP and t-SNE) and clustering to automatically identify similarities within the encoded data.

Each point is a sample of handwriting that has been encoded by the model and positioned based on similarity to other samples by UMAP or t-SNE, which are common techniques for visualising high-dimensional data. Points (styles) that are more similar will be positioned nearer on the plot. The axis are the embedding dimensions of the algorithms and each algorithm has unique characteristics which may result in different groupings (e.g. UMAP preserves global structure/similarity more strongly than t-SNE).

Points have also been coloured according to similarities found by unsupervised clustering algorithms.

Whilst by no means a perfect separation, we can see that some points found closer together share similarities in style and although we don't know which exact features the model finds informative towards assessing similarity, we can see that these might include curliness or thickness of writing, for example.

We've also included some well-known classical fonts, such as Times New Roman, to see whether any styles align closely with them.

The code for the visualisation is on GitHub.
